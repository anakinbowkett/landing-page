Angle Bisectors

Cutting an angle exactly in half using a compass and ruler

What This Is

An angle bisector is a straight line that cuts an angle into two equal halves. When you bisect an angle, each half is exactly the same size as the other. You draw an angle bisector using a compass — no measuring in degrees, no protractor. The compass arcs do all the work, and the result is always perfectly accurate.

Key Terms You Must Know

Angle Bisector

A line that divides an angle into two equal halves. "Bisect" means to cut into two equal parts.

Vertex

The corner point where two lines of the angle meet. You always place your compass point here first.

Arc

A curved line drawn with a compass — a part of a circle. Arcs are how you mark equal distances.

Compass

A drawing tool with a sharp point and a pencil arm. You use it to draw arcs that are always the same distance from a point.

Intersection

The point where two arcs cross each other. Your bisector line always passes through this crossing point.

The Method — Step by Step
Grade 1–3 topic. You will need a compass, a pencil, and a ruler. No protractor is needed — the compass does the measuring for you.
1

Place your compass on the vertex and draw a big arc across both arms

Put your compass point exactly on the vertex — the corner where the two arms of the angle meet. This is the only starting position that works, because every arc you draw must be the same distance from that corner.

Open your compass to any comfortable width — roughly one third of the arm length works well. Keeping the compass point fixed on the vertex, draw a single arc that crosses both arms of the angle. This arc marks two points that are exactly the same distance from the vertex, one on each arm.

Example: For an 80° angle, place your compass on the vertex and sweep an arc that nicks both lines — you now have two small blue dots, one on each arm of the angle.
2

Move your compass to one of the intersection points and draw a second arc inside the angle

Lift your compass and place the point on one of the two dots where your first arc crossed an arm. Do not change the compass width — keep it exactly as it was in Step 1. This equal width is what guarantees the bisector will be perfectly in the middle.

Draw a short arc inside the angle — roughly in the middle space between the two arms. You are not trying to hit anything yet; you are just putting an arc in the interior of the angle ready for the next move.

Example: Place your compass on the upper intersection dot and draw a short arc that curves into the open space of the 80° angle.
3

Move your compass to the other intersection point and draw a third arc so it crosses the second one

Now place your compass point on the other dot — the one on the second arm. The compass width stays identical to Step 1 and Step 2. You are using the same radius for all three arcs throughout this whole construction.

Draw another arc inside the angle so that it crosses the arc you drew in Step 2. Where these two arcs cross, you get one clear intersection point. That crossing point is equidistant from both arms of the angle — it sits exactly in the middle.

Example: Place your compass on the lower dot (on the horizontal arm), draw an arc into the interior — it will cross your Step 2 arc to give one clear crossing point.
4

Draw a straight line from the vertex through the intersection point — that is your bisector

Pick up your ruler. Place it so the edge passes through both the vertex and the point where the two arcs crossed in Step 3. These two points define the direction of your bisector — always draw the line through both of them.

Draw a solid line from the vertex through the crossing point and extend it a little beyond. This line is your angle bisector — it divides the original angle into two angles that are exactly equal to each other. Each half is half of the original angle.

Example: Draw a line from the vertex of the 80° angle through the crossing point of the arcs. Both halves now measure 40°.
5

In the event of a right angle — the method is identical, but the answer is always 45°

If the question gives you a right angle (a 90° angle, often shown with a small square in the corner), use the exact same four steps above. The compass construction does not change at all — it works for any angle.

The question might also tell you the angle in degrees and ask you to verify your bisector is correct by measuring with a protractor. If it does, each half must measure exactly half the original angle — measure both halves and check they are equal.

Example: If asked to bisect an 80° angle and verify, your bisector should produce two angles of 40° each — measure both with a protractor to confirm.
