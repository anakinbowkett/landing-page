Time Calculations

Finding the gap between two times — and converting between hours, minutes and seconds

What This Is

Time calculations ask you to find the gap between a start time and an end time, or to add or subtract a duration from a given time. The tricky part is that time does not work like ordinary numbers — there are 60 minutes in an hour, not 100, so you cannot subtract times the way you subtract decimals. This guide teaches you to count on in stages so you never go wrong with the 60-minute boundary.

Key Terms You Must Know

Duration

The length of time between a start and an end. Always expressed as hours and minutes (or minutes and seconds) — never as a decimal unless the question specifically asks for it.

24-hour clock

A way of writing times that runs from 00:00 (midnight) to 23:59. There is no am or pm — 14:30 means 2:30 in the afternoon. All four digits are always written.

12-hour clock

A way of writing times that uses the numbers 1–12 twice, with am (before noon) and pm (after noon) to tell them apart. 2:30 pm and 14:30 are the same moment.

Count-on method

A strategy for finding the gap between two times by jumping in stages to a convenient hour first, then counting the remaining minutes. It avoids subtracting across the 60-minute boundary.

Converting time

Changing a duration from one unit to another. To convert minutes to hours and minutes, divide by 60 — the quotient is the hours and the remainder is the minutes left over.

The Method — Step by Step
Grade 1–3 topic. Time calculations appear on every Foundation paper. The count-on method works for any pair of times — even ones that cross midnight.
1

Identify the start time and the end time clearly

Read the question and write the start time and the end time on separate lines before doing any calculation. Writing them down separately stops you from mixing them up mid-working, which is the single most common source of errors on time questions.

Check whether the times are given in 12-hour or 24-hour format. If they are in 12-hour format, convert both to 24-hour format before you start — working in a single consistent format removes the risk of a pm time being treated as an am time.

Start: 09:47  |  End: 13:15
2

Count on from the start time to the next whole hour

Look at the minutes of the start time and work out how many minutes are left until the next complete hour. For 09:47, the next whole hour is 10:00. The gap is 60 − 47 = 13 minutes. Write this down as your first jump.

This first jump always lands you on a clean whole hour, which makes every jump after it much simpler. You are not doing a complicated subtraction — you are doing a small addition to reach a friendly number, exactly as you would on a number line for any ordinary arithmetic.

09:47 → 10:00  (+13 minutes)
3

Count on in whole hours until you are as close to the end time as possible

From the whole hour you reached in Step 2, count forward in full hours until you reach the hour that is just before (or equal to) the end time. For end time 13:15, you count on from 10:00 in whole hours: 10:00 → 11:00 → 12:00 → 13:00. That is 3 complete hours.

Stop your hour-counting when the next full hour would overshoot the end time. Here, 14:00 would go past 13:15, so you stop at 13:00. Keep a running total of the time accumulated so far: 13 minutes from Step 2 plus 3 hours from this step.

10:00 → 13:00  (+3 hours)  Running total: 3 hr 13 min
4

Count on the remaining minutes to reach the exact end time

Look at the minutes of the end time. You are currently at 13:00 and the end time is 13:15, so you need 15 more minutes. Add those minutes to your running total. This final jump is always a straightforward whole-number addition — there is no crossing of an hour boundary because you deliberately stopped at 13:00.

Add all three jumps together to get the total duration: 13 minutes + 3 hours + 15 minutes. Combine the minutes first: 13 + 15 = 28 minutes. Then write the final answer as hours and minutes in the form the question requires.

13:00 → 13:15  (+15 min)  Total: 3 hours 28 minutes
5

In the event of… the minutes total reaching 60 or more

If the minutes you collect across your jumps add up to 60 or more, you must convert the excess into an extra hour. Divide the total minutes by 60 — the quotient tells you how many extra hours to carry over, and the remainder is the minutes left. For example, if your jumps give 45 + 35 = 80 minutes and 2 hours, convert: 80 ÷ 60 = 1 remainder 20, so the total becomes 3 hours 20 minutes.

This situation arises when both the start time and end time have large minute values — for example 08:45 to 11:35. The first jump is 15 minutes, the last jump is 35 minutes, giving 50 minutes total alongside the 3 hours, which is under 60 — no conversion needed. Always check the minutes total before writing your final answer.

If minutes total ≥ 60: divide by 60, carry the quotient to hours, keep the remainder as minutes.

✗−A1
Column subtraction: 15 − 47 needs borrowing from hours, giving 75 − 47 = 28 minutes — but column method gives 68 when applied directly, which is impossible since 68 > 60.

68 minutes does not exist — there are only 60 minutes in an hour!

0 / 3 marks
Subtracting time like ordinary numbers produces impossible answers. There are 60 minutes in an hour, not 100 — column subtraction of the minutes digits ignores this entirely. When 47 is subtracted from 15 using borrowing, the borrowed unit is worth 60 minutes, not 10. Use the count-on method instead: jump to the next whole hour, count complete hours, then add the remaining minutes. You will never produce an impossible minutes value this way.
