Product of Prime Factors

Breaking any integer down into its prime building blocks — and writing the result in index notation

What This Is

Every whole number greater than 1 can be broken down into a unique set of prime numbers multiplied together — this is called its prime factorisation. You use a factor tree to find those primes systematically, splitting each branch until every tip holds a prime number. The final answer is written in index notation, grouping any repeated primes as powers. This skill underpins HCF, LCM, and several number-theory questions across the GCSE paper.

Key Terms You Must Know

Prime number

A whole number greater than 1 that has exactly two factors: 1 and itself. Examples: 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13.

Composite number

A whole number greater than 1 that has more than two factors — it can be broken down further. Examples: 4, 6, 15, 21.

Factor tree

A branching diagram where you repeatedly split a number into two factors until every branch tip is a prime number.

Index notation

A way of writing repeated multiplication as a power. For example, 2 × 2 × 2 is written as 2³. The small raised number is the index.

Product of prime factors

The final expression showing the original number written as primes multiplied together in index notation, e.g. 60 = 2² × 3 × 5.

The Method — Step by Step
Grade 4–5 topic. A reliable 3-mark question at the start of the number section. Master the tree and the index notation line and you will pick these marks up every time.
1

Write your number at the top and draw two branches

Start by writing the number you need to factorise at the top of your page. Draw two branches coming down from it. You are about to split it into any two factors — it does not matter which pair you choose because you will always reach exactly the same set of primes in the end. This is the foundation of the whole method, so write it clearly.

Write 60 at the top. Draw two branches below it.
2

Choose a factor pair and write one factor on each branch

Choose any pair of factors that multiply to make your number. Most students use 2 and half the number when the number is even — that is a reliable starting point. Write one factor at the end of each branch. If a factor is prime, circle it and stop that branch. If it is not prime, draw two more branches from it and split it again.

Split 60 into 2 × 30. Circle 2 (prime). Draw branches from 30.
3

Keep splitting every composite until all tips are prime

Continue splitting every composite number at the end of a branch. Each time you reach a prime — 2, 3, 5, 7, 11 — circle it and stop that branch. Keep going until every single branch tip is circled. The tree is only finished when there are no un-circled numbers left at any tip. This is what earns the method marks on the AQA mark scheme.

30 = 2 × 15, then 15 = 3 × 5. Circle 2, 3, and 5.
4

Read off all the circled primes in order from smallest to largest

Read off every circled prime from the bottom of your tree. Write them all down in order from smallest to largest. This list is your raw material for the final answer. Getting the full list correct — with every prime included and none repeated — is what unlocks the accuracy mark. Check against your tree: one circled number produces one entry in the list.

Circled primes from 60: 2, 2, 3, 5 — list all four.
5

Group repeated primes as powers and write the final answer in index notation

Look at your list of primes and group any that repeat. Two 2s become 2², three 3s become 3³ — write the base number with the count as the index. Primes that appear once stay as they are. Write the final expression as the original number equals the product of all the prime powers. This final line — written correctly in index notation — is what the AQA mark scheme awards the A1 for.

Two 2s → 2²; one 3 → 3; one 5 → 5. Answer: 60 = 2² × 3 × 5.
