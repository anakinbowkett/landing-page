Stem-and-Leaf Diagrams

Reading, drawing and using back-to-back diagrams to compare two sets of data

What This Is

A stem-and-leaf diagram is a way of organising numbers so you can see every single value and spot patterns at the same time. You split each number into a stem (usually the tens digit) and a leaf (the units digit), then line them up in order. The diagram keeps all the original data — unlike a bar chart, nothing is lost. Examiners use it to test whether you can read off the median, mode and range directly from the ordered list.

Key Terms You Must Know

Stem

The leading digit(s) of each value, written once in the middle column. For two-digit numbers the stem is the tens digit; for three-digit numbers it is the hundreds and tens digits.

Leaf

The final digit of each value, written to the right of the stem (or left, in a back-to-back diagram). Every data point produces exactly one leaf.

Key

The legend that tells the reader how to decode the diagram. Always written as an example, e.g. 2 | 3 means 23. Without a key the diagram cannot be read — AQA expects it every time.

Back-to-back diagram

A diagram comparing two groups. Both share a central stem column. One group's leaves go to the right; the other group's leaves go to the left, reading outward from the stem.

Ordered / unordered

Leaves must be written in ascending order from the stem outward. An unordered diagram is incomplete — you cannot find the median from it until the leaves are sorted.

The Method — Step by Step
1
Find the stems

Look at your data and find the smallest and largest values. The stems are the tens digits that cover that entire range — one row per tens value.

Write the stems in a vertical column from smallest at the top to largest at the bottom. Draw a vertical line to the right of the stem column — leaves go on that side.

e.g. For the data 12, 25, 31, 18, 27, 34, 22, 15 — the values go from 12 to 34, so the stems are 1, 2, 3.
2
Add the leaves (unordered first)

Go through the data in the order it appears. For each number, find its stem row and write its units digit as a leaf to the right of the line. Do this for every single value — never skip one.

Now reorder the leaves in each row from smallest to largest, reading left to right away from the stem. This ordered form is the completed diagram — an unordered diagram will lose marks.

e.g. 12, 18, 15 all go in the 1 row → unordered: 2 8 5. Reorder → 2 5 8. Every leaf must appear — count them to check.
3
Write the key

Below the diagram write a key that shows one example of how to read a stem and leaf together. The key must combine one actual stem with one actual leaf and give the full number it represents.

The key is not optional — AQA always awards a mark for it. Without it, a reader cannot tell whether the diagram uses tens, hundreds or decimals as stems.

e.g. For our diagram — Key: 1 | 2 means 12. Now any reader can reconstruct every data value from the diagram alone.
4
Read off statistics from the completed diagram

Count all the leaves to find n (the number of values). The median is the middle value when n is odd: position = (n + 1) ÷ 2. When n is even, average the two middle values.

The range is the largest value minus the smallest. Both values sit at the ends of the ordered list — largest leaf on the bottom row, smallest leaf on the top row.

e.g. n = 8 values. Median position = (8+1)÷2 = 4.5th value. 4th value = 22, 5th value = 25. Median = (22+25)÷2 = 23.5. Range = 34 − 12 = 22.
5
In the event of a back-to-back diagram — read leaves outward from the stem

In a back-to-back diagram, Group A's leaves appear to the left of the stem and Group B's leaves appear to the right. The stem sits in the centre and is shared by both groups.

To read a Group A value, combine the stem with the leaf reading toward the stem — the leaf closest to the divider is the smallest. For Group B, read left to right as normal. Both groups need their own ordered leaves.

e.g. In Group A, the row "8 4 | 2 |" means values 24 and 28, written outward from the stem. Group B in the same row "| 2 | 1 7" means 21 and 27.
