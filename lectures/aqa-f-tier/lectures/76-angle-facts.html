Angle Facts — AQA Foundation GCSE

Angle Facts

AQA Foundation GCSE  ·  Topic 76

Key Terms
Straight Line
Angles on one side of a straight line add up to 180°.
Full Turn
All the angles meeting at a single point add up to 360°.
Vertically Opposite
When two lines cross, the angles directly opposite each other are always equal.
Interior Angle Sum
Angles inside a triangle always add up to 180°.
Exterior Angle
An angle outside a triangle, formed by extending one side, equals the sum of the two opposite interior angles.
The Four Rules
Rule 1 — Angles on a Straight Line
a + b = 180°
Any angles that sit on one side of a straight line — sharing the same vertex — must add up to exactly 180°. Use this rule to find a missing angle by subtracting what you know from 180.
Rule 2 — Angles Around a Point
a + b + c + d = 360°
All angles meeting at a single point, going all the way around, add up to 360°. This is a full rotation. Count every angle you can see, add them up, and subtract from 360 to find any missing one.
Rule 3 — Vertically Opposite Angles
opposite angles are equal
When two straight lines cross, they form four angles. The two angles directly opposite each other are always equal. Also, any two angles next to each other (a pair that shares an arm) must add up to 180°, because they sit on a straight line.
Rule 4 — Angles in a Triangle
a + b + c = 180°
The three interior angles of any triangle — however big or small, however pointy or flat — always add up to exactly 180°. This lets you find a missing angle by subtracting the two known angles from 180.

The exterior angle (red) equals the sum of the two non-adjacent interior angles: 55° + 55° = 110°

How to Find a Missing Angle — Step by Step
1
Look at the diagram and identify which rule applies.
Start by reading the diagram carefully. Count how many lines meet at the vertex where the unknown angle sits. If the angles share a straight line, use Rule 1 (sum to 180°). If they all meet at a point with multiple lines, use Rule 2 (sum to 360°). If two full straight lines cross, use Rule 3 (vertically opposite). If you can see a triangle, use Rule 4 (interior angles sum to 180°). Picking the right rule is the entire skill — the arithmetic that follows is just subtraction.
2
Write down the rule as an equation with the unknown on one side.
Write the equation the rule gives you — for example, if two angles are on a straight line write x + 47 = 180. Always write this line of working because the mark scheme awards a method mark for showing the correct angle relationship, even if your arithmetic goes wrong afterwards. The equation tells the examiner you understand the geometry. Do not jump straight to the answer.
3
Subtract the known angles from the total to find the unknown.
Rearrange your equation so that x is on its own. For a straight line: x = 180 − 47 = 133°. For angles in a triangle: subtract both known angles from 180. For angles around a point: subtract all known angles from 360. Show the subtraction as a separate written line. Never do this step in your head and skip writing it — examiners cannot award a mark they cannot see.
4
State the final answer with the degree symbol.
Write your answer clearly with the ° symbol — for example, x = 133°. The degree symbol is not optional: leaving it out is a real reason to lose the accuracy mark on AQA papers. Underline or box your final answer so it stands out. If the question asks you to give a reason, write the name of the rule in full — "angles on a straight line" or "angles in a triangle" — not just "straight line rule."
Worked Example
AQA WORKED EXAMPLE
The diagram shows two straight lines crossing. One angle is 63°. Find the values of angles p, q, and r.
Rule: two straight lines cross → use straight line (180°) and vertically opposite
p + 63° = 180°M1 (angles on a straight line)
p = 180° − 63° = 117°A1
q = 63°A1 (vertically opposite to 63°)
r = 117°A1 (vertically opposite to p)
Check: 63 + 117 + 63 + 117 = 360° ✓
p = 117°, q = 63°, r = 117°
Common Mistakes to Avoid
AQA COMMON ERROR 1
A student is told two angles on a straight line are 47° and x. They write: x = 360 − 47 = 313°
x = 360 − 47 = 313° ✗ A1 lost
Should be: x + 47 = 180
x = 180 − 47 = 133° A1

Straight lines use 180°, not 360°.

The angles are on one side of a straight line, not all the way around a point. A straight line is half a full turn — it spans exactly 180°. The rule 360° applies only when angles completely surround a point on all sides. Whenever the angles share a straight line, subtract from 180. Never subtract from 360 unless you can see all the way around the vertex.
AQA COMMON ERROR 2
Two lines cross. One angle is 58°. A student says the angle directly next to it (sharing an arm) is also 58° because "opposite angles are equal."
adjacent = 58° (wrong — not opposite) ✗
Opposite (across the X) = 58° ✓
Adjacent (next to it) = 180−58 = 122° A1

"Opposite" means across the X, not next door.

Vertically opposite means directly across the crossing point — the angle on the other side of the X. The angle right next to a given angle is not its opposite; it is supplementary, and you must subtract from 180°. Remember: adjacent angles at a crossing add to 180°, opposite angles are equal. Draw an X and label the four zones — it is immediately obvious which pairs are truly opposite.
AQA COMMON ERROR 3
A triangle has angles 72°, 65°, and y. A student writes: y = 360 − 72 − 65 = 223°
y = 360 − 72 − 65 = 223° ✗ A1 lost
Triangle angles sum to 180°:
y = 180 − 72 − 65 = 43° A1

Triangles sum to 180°, not 360°.

Angles inside a triangle sum to 180°, not 360°. The 360° rule is for angles meeting at a single point on a flat surface going all the way around. A triangle has three angles that together make exactly one straight-line's worth of turn — 180°. Whenever you see a triangle, the total is always 180. Subtract both known angles from 180, never from 360.
How AQA Phrases This Topic
AQA
"Find the value of x. Give reasons for your answer." — you must name the rule, not just give the number.
AQA
"Angle ABC = 47°. Find angle CBD." — the letters name the vertex (middle letter) and the two arms. Visualise the arms before applying a rule.
AQA
"Show that the angles in the triangle add up to 180°." — you must write a clear chain of working; a single number earns zero.
AQA
"Work out the exterior angle of the triangle." — remember: exterior = sum of the two non-adjacent interior angles.
AQA
"Not drawn accurately." — this means you cannot measure the diagram with a protractor. You must calculate using the angle rules.
Check Yourself
Q1 — Draw It
On paper, draw two straight lines crossing at a point. Label one angle 48°. Without looking at this guide, mark and calculate all four angles at the crossing. Write the rule you used for each one.
Q2 — Write the Rules
Without looking at this guide, write down all four angle rules from memory. For each one, write the total the angles add up to and one situation where you would use it.
Q3 — Spot the Error
A student sees a triangle with angles 80° and 60°. They write: the missing angle = 360 − 80 − 60 = 220°. Write down exactly what is wrong with this answer and give the correct value.
Q4 — Answer This
Two straight lines cross. One of the four angles is 131°. Find the other three angles. Give a reason for each answer. [4 marks]
