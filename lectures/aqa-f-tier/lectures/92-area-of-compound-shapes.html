Area of Compound Shapes

Split any shape into rectangles, find each area, then add them together — the complete AQA method

What This Is

A compound shape is a shape made by joining two or more simple shapes together. You cannot find its area in one go — but you can split it into rectangles, find the area of each rectangle separately, and then add the areas together. You already know how to find the area of a rectangle: length × width. This topic is that skill, used twice.

Key Terms You Must Know

Compound shape

A shape made by joining two or more simpler shapes. It has an unusual outline — not a plain rectangle or square on its own.

Area

The amount of flat space inside a shape. Measured in square units: cm², m², mm². Area = length × width for a rectangle.

Missing length

A side whose measurement is not labelled. You work it out by subtracting or adding the lengths you are given on the opposite side of the shape.

Split line

The line you draw across the shape to cut it into two rectangles. You choose where to draw it — any valid cut is acceptable.

cm²

Square centimetres — the unit for area when lengths are in centimetres. Always write the unit in your final answer or the accuracy mark is lost.

The Method — Step by Step
Grade 3–4 topic. Every compound shape question gives you a shape with some side lengths labelled. Your job is always the same: split, find each area, add them together.
1

Look at the shape and decide where to draw a split line to make two rectangles

Look at the outline of the shape. Find the inner corner — the corner that points inward, making the shape look like a letter L or T. Draw a straight line across that corner to cut the shape into two plain rectangles. You choose whether to cut horizontally or vertically — either way works, as long as both pieces are proper rectangles.

Once you draw the split line, you should see exactly two rectangles. Label them Rectangle A and Rectangle B on your diagram. This keeps your working clear and stops you mixing up which area belongs to which piece.

For the worked example shape, draw a horizontal line across at the inner corner. This gives Rectangle A on top and Rectangle B below.
2

Find any missing side length by subtracting

Look at the side lengths you are given. There is always one side that is not labelled. Find which side it is, then look at the opposite side of the shape — the lengths on the opposite side must add up to the unlabelled one. Subtract the shorter length from the longer one to find the missing value.

Write the missing length onto your diagram before doing any area calculations. You need every side labelled before you can find the areas — an unlabelled side means you cannot complete the question.

Left side = 8 cm total. Top piece of right side = 4 cm. Missing bottom piece of right side = 8 − 4 = 4 cm. Write 4 cm on that side.
3

Find the area of Rectangle A: length × width

Read off the two side lengths of Rectangle A from your diagram. Multiply length × width. This gives you the area of Rectangle A in cm². Write it clearly as "Area A = … cm²".

Rectangle A: 10 cm wide, 4 cm tall. Area A = 10 × 4 = 40 cm².
4

Find the area of Rectangle B: length × width

Now do the same for Rectangle B. Read its two side lengths from your diagram — including any missing length you calculated in Step 2. Multiply length × width and write "Area B = … cm²".

Rectangle B: 6 cm wide, 4 cm tall (the missing length we worked out). Area B = 6 × 4 = 24 cm².
5

Add the two areas together and write the final answer with the unit

Add Area A and Area B together. This gives the total area of the whole compound shape. Write the full equation: Area A + Area B = total. The total must include the unit — cm², m², or mm² depending on what the question uses.

Write the final answer on its own line as "Total area = 64 cm²". The accuracy mark (A1) on AQA requires both the correct number and the correct unit. A correct number without cm² loses the A1.

40 + 24 = 64. Total area = 64 cm². Always write cm² at the end — never write "64" on its own.
6

In the event of a subtraction method — take the missing piece away from the big rectangle

Some compound shapes are quicker to handle by imagining a complete big rectangle and then subtracting the missing corner. Spot this when it is obvious the shape is a rectangle with a rectangular chunk cut out from one corner. This method gives exactly the same answer as the split method — it is the faster route for that specific shape.

Calculate the area of the full big rectangle first. Then calculate the area of the missing rectangular corner. Subtract the corner from the big rectangle. The answer is identical to the split method — so use whichever approach you find clearer.

Big rectangle = 10 × 8 = 80 cm². Missing corner = 4 × 4 = 16 cm². Total area = 80 − 16 = 64 cm². Identical to the split method answer.
